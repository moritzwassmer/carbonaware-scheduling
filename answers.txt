1) Since this is an artificial scenario, you can simply assume that each active pod 
consumes 200W of electricity. As workloads run 40s on average and we have 
180 workloads in total, the expected overall energy usage is40s * 180 * 200W 
= 400 Wh. What is the carbon footprint? 

    Assuming the mentioned "intensity" is defined as g CO2eq/kWh (since not explicitely specified) the avg. carbon footprint is:
    Energy consumption: 400 Wh = 0.4 kWh
    avg. Footprint normal: 0.4 kWh * 290.72 g CO2eq/kWh = 116.29 g C02eg
    avg. Footprint carbon: 0.4 kWh * 212.86 g CO2eq/kWh = 85.14 g C02eg

3) Have there been situations where a Pod was not placed as intended? If so, what 
might be the reasons? If not, is this even possible? 

    There were no misplacements in our experiments. We used the 
    requiredDuringSchedulingIgnoredDuringExecution type Node affinity,
    which means that the scheduler can't schedule the Pod 
    unless the rule is met. Therefore, it can't be misplaced. 

    In theory it can happen that the resources of the selected node are insufficient, 
    especially when the scheduling frequency and workload volume is high.
    That means that the scheduler might not be able to assign the workload to it spreferred pod. 
    In that case it is likely that the pod is going to be in status 'Pending'
    until a sufficient amount of resources is free again.


4) Think about applying this in practice: What might go wrong, what are things 
we did not take into consideration? 

    -   Often businesses prefer lower costs over carbon footprint (trivial but reality)
    -   In practice, workloads may have varying priorities, execution times, and resource requirements. 
        Also many workloads need multiple pods that need to communicate with each other. if nodes are
        distributed over large distances, latency could become become a performance bottleneck.
    -   If scheduling frequency and workloads are high, queues for pod placement for nodes could get very big with
        the strict scheduling strategy described in 3). By the time a pod is running, the carbon footprint could be changed.
        Therefore the pod should be redistributed.
    -   Nodes in our clusters could have different hardware configurations, affecting energy consumption and carbon intensity.
    -   For some data intensive workloads it might be beneficial to execute them close to where the data should be stored, or 
        close to where the data resides.
    -   This task did also not consider any fault tolerance. It is also possible that pods fail.
    -   Some workloads might have legal or regulatory constraints requiring them to run in specific geographical regions.
    -   Carbon intensity forecasts might not always be accurate, especially in regions with volatile energy grids 
        (e.g., high reliance on solar or wind energy).
